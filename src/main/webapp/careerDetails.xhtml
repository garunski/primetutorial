Overview

Actors play parts in stage, television, radio, video, or film productions, or other settings for entertainment, information, or instruction. They interpret serious or comic roles by speech, gesture, and body movement to entertain or inform audiences. Many work in films, theaters, television shows, commercials, and other performing arts venues.

Acting requires a wide range of skills, including the ability to memorize lines, understand dramatic techniques, work with directors and other actors, and perform in front of an audience or camera.

Tasks &amp; Responsibilities

The list below outlines occupation specific tasks that a worker in this occupation is called upon to do regularly.

  • Collaborate with other actors as part of an ensemble
  • Portray and interpret roles, using speech, gestures, and body movements, to entertain, inform, or instruct radio, film, television, or live audiences
  • Work closely with directors, other actors, and playwrights to find the interpretation most suited to the role
  • Perform humorous and serious interpretations of emotions, actions, and situations, using body movements, facial expressions, and gestures
  • Study and rehearse roles from scripts to interpret, learn and memorize lines, stunts, and cues as directed
  • Learn about characters in scripts and their relationships to each other to develop role interpretations
  • Attend auditions and casting calls to audition for roles
  • Sing or dance during dramatic or comedic performances
  • Work with other crew members responsible for lighting, costumes, make-up, and props
  • Tell jokes, perform comic dances, songs and skits, impersonate mannerisms and voices of others
  • Read from scripts or books to narrate action or to inform or entertain audiences
  • Promote productions using means such as interviews about plays or movies

Work Environment

Actors work in various settings, including theaters, movie studios, theme parks, or on location. Their work may involve:

  • Working with a team or ensemble
  • Irregular work schedules, often including evenings, weekends, and holidays
  • Periods of unemployment between projects
  • Physical demands, including standing for long periods and performing physically demanding tasks
  • Travel for performances or filming
  • High levels of competition for roles

Educational Paths

Many actors enhance their skills through formal dramatic education. Common educational paths include:

  • High school drama classes
  • Bachelor's degree in theater arts, drama, or related field
  • Master of Fine Arts (MFA) in acting
  • Acting conservatories or specialized acting schools
  • Workshops and continuing education classes

School Programs

The following programs are applicable to this occupation:

  • Drama and Dramatics/Theatre Arts
  • Acting
  • Directing and Theatrical Production
  • Playwriting and Screenwriting
  • Theatre/Theatre Arts Management
  • Dramatic/Theatre Arts and Stagecraft

Many actors also gain experience through community theater, school productions, or acting workshops before pursuing professional roles. Practical experience is highly valued in this field.

Salary &amp; Job Outlook

Salary Information

In New York State, the average annual wage for actors is:

  • Entry level: $44,630
  • Experienced: $116,730

Wages shown here are calculated from an average hourly rate paid full time over a one year period. As these positions do not typically follow a 40-hour work week for the duration of a year, actual annual salaries may vary widely.

Job Outlook

Employment prospects for actors in New York State are described as Very Favorable.

  • During 2018, there were approximately 7,660 Actors employed in New York State.
  • By 2030, this number is estimated to grow to 11,120, representing an increase of 346 jobs each year.
  • Total annual job openings: 1,362
